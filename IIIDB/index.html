About the IIIDB


Nowadays, many protein-protein interaction (PPI) databases are available, but the isoform level PPI prediction database has not been seen yet. Thus, we developed the first isoform-isoform interaction database, IIIDB (http://syslab.nchu.edu.tw/IIIDB). The interactions in IIIDB were calculated by a logistic regression approach that integrates information from RNA-seq datasets, orthology, domain-domain interaction, and GO annotation.

Tutorial

In IIIDB, the users can easily access high-confidence and low-confidence predictions of human IIIs, and then see the full evidence values for each predicted III (Figure 1). The IIIDB allows users to easily search IIIs and isoform modules, and then provides the evidence that led to each III prediction. Interestingly, the different isoforms within the same gene may be involved with different isoform modules that may open a new door to study differential functionality of isoforms of the gene. The IIIDB also provides the comprehensive enrichment analysis results (GO, pathway, and complex) for isoform modules, which may help biologists to study the biological insight of network modules in isoform level.

In the interaction search section (the red dashed line box), users can search high-confidence (score > 3) and low-confidence (score > 2) predictions. (B) The predicted III table shows full evidence values including average co-expression (Pearson correlation coefficient), domain interaction score, GO similarity score, orthology score, and PubMed co-citation. (C) The result of module searching includes the network graph, complex enrichment, pathway enrichment, and GO enrichment. If a network graph is more complex and compact, users can click a full figure link for a larger high-resolution image.
  
     

III search and isoform module search function in IIIDB. The searching function has three parts: high-confidence interaction prediction search, low-confidence interaction prediction search, and isoform module search. The IIIDB provides auto-complete function in all search functions (Figure 2). Users can input gene symbol or gene ID into the auto-complete field which is provide quickly finding and selecting some match value. Furthermore, by entering more characters, it will filter down the list to better matches. The auto-complete function not only helps the users search efficiently but also makes a quick filtering.

To obtain the isoform annotations in human genome, we used the UCSC KnownGene database, which includes 66,803 human isoforms (KnownGenes) based on the hg18 assembly. We have assembled a collection of proteomic and genomic data sources for predicting III (See Figure 3) as follows: (1) RNA-seq data: We collected 10 RNA-seq datasets, each with sample size  10 (Supplementary Table 1). Pearson’s expression correlations for all isoform pairs were derived from each dataset. We hypothesize that two frequently co-expressed isoforms are more likely to interact than those that are not. (2) Orthology information: We performed BLAT between all human isoforms and proteins of other species to identify sequence homologs. We suspect that the PPIs of other species may suggest interactions among homolog human proteins. Given two protein sequences a and a', we calculated global homology (a, a') = (length of the common subsequence) / (maximum length of a and a'). If two human isoform pair a and b have orthologus protein pair a' and b', which has a PPI in the other species in IntAct database, we assign orthology score = (homology(a, a') + homology(b, b'))/2 to the isoform pair a and b. (3) Domain-domain interaction data: if the domains of two isoforms have interactions in the DOMINE database, we assign the domain score for the two isoforms as follows: high-confidence prediction: 3, medium-confidence prediction: 2, and low-confidence prediction: 1. (4) Similarities of GO annotations: Previous studies showed that proteins with shared GO functions are more likely to interact. Here, for any two isoforms, we estimate their functional similarity by the smallest shared biological process (SSBP) of their respective parent genes, and then take 10/SSBP as GO similarity score. This is only an approximation, since an isoform may not have all functions of its parent gene.
